Baursak

Description

More than just an everyday staple, Kazakh baursak is a puffy fried bread prepared for major life events, including weddings and memorials. As it cooks, the fragrant steam is said to carry high into the heavens, offering a symbolic taste to ancestors so they can celebrate or mourn alongside the living.

Adapt this recipe to your liking by adjusting the sweetness, choosing between butter or oil, or adding an egg for a richer dough. No matter the twist, you will produce perfectly fluffy, hollow pillows of bread that are crisp yet soft and completely addictive. For a savory batch, just reduce the sugar to a single pinch.

Ingridients

  • 1.5kg white flour
  • 2 eggs
  • 500ml milk
  • 10g dried yeast
  • 1 table spoon of melted butter
  • 1 table spoon of salt
  • 4 table spoons of sugar

How to cook:

  1. In a small bowl, combine the 500ml of lightly warmed milk, 10g of dried yeast, and 1 tbsp of the sugar (taken from your total amount). Stir gently and let it sit for about 10 minutes until it becomes frothy on top. Make sure the milk is just warm to the touch, not hot, or it will kill the yeast.
  2. In a large mixing bowl, whisk together the 2 eggs, the remaining sugar, 1 tbsp of salt, and 1 tbsp of melted butter (make sure the butter has cooled slightly so it doesn't cook the eggs). Pour the frothy yeast-milk mixture into this bowl and stir to combine.
  3. Sift the 1.5kg of white flour and begin adding it to the liquid mixture gradually, stirring with a wooden spoon. Once it becomes too thick to spoon, use your hands to knead it inside the bowl or on a lightly floured surface. Knead for about 10 minutes until the dough is smooth, elastic, and no longer sticks to your hands.
  4. Shape the dough into a ball and place it back into a clean, lightly oiled bowl. Cover the bowl with a clean kitchen towel or plastic wrap. Place it in a warm, draft-free spot for 1 to 1.5 hours, or until it has doubled in size.
  5. Punch down the risen dough and divide it into 3 or 4 manageable portions. Take one portion and roll it out on a lightly floured surface to a thickness of about 7mm to 1cm (just under half an inch). Cut the dough into your preferred shape: either into traditional diamonds/rectangles or circles (you can use the rim of a small glass). Cover the cut pieces with a towel and let them rest for 15 minutes to puff up slightly before frying.
  6. Pour enough vegetable oil into a deep pan, wok, or heavy-bottomed pot (like a kazan) so the baursaks can float freely, and heat it over medium-high heat. You can test the oil by dropping a small piece of dough in - if it bubbles instantly and rises to the top, the oil is ready. Fry the baursaks in batches—do not overcrowd the pot. They will puff up rapidly into little pillows. Keep them moving gently with a slotted spoon so they brown evenly. Fry for about 3 minutes until they are a beautiful golden brown all over.
  7. Remove the baursaks with a slotted spoon and place them on a plate lined with paper towels to drain any excess oil.
  8. Pile them high on a large platter while they are still warm. Serve alongside a steaming pot of Kazakh black tea with milk, fresh cream (kaimak), or jam. Enjoy!
