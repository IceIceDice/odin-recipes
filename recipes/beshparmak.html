Beshparmak

Description

It is the national dish of Kazakhstan, a culinary masterpiece of nomadic tradition, and the ultimate ultimate centerpiece for any major celebration. The name literally translates to "Five Fingers," because it was traditionally eaten by hand. It is a brilliant lesson in simplicity, relying on high-quality ingredients and patience rather than complex spices.

Serving Beshbarmak is a deeply ritualistic. Traditionally, a guest of honor would select an animal from the herd, and the entire sheep — including the head — would be slow-boiled for the feast. The meal was served family-style on large communal platters, with everyone seated on the floor or at a low table. The most respected elder would carve the head, distributing specific cuts according to a traditional social hierarchy based on age, gender, and rank. While elders received the prime pieces, children were given smaller portions as a symbolic lesson, encouraging them to work hard to earn their own respected place in society.

Ingridients

  • Lamb (or any beef)
  • Zhaima (or Lasagna noodles)
  • White onions
  • Potatoes
  • Salt
  • Pepper

How to cook:

  1. To build the foundation of your dish, place the bone-in lamb cuts into a large, deep stockpot and cover them completely with about three to four liters of cold water. Bring the pot to a vigorous boil over high heat, keeping a close eye on it as a thick, gray foam rises to the surface. It is essential to diligently skim off all of this residue with a spoon or mesh strainer to guarantee your final broth (sorpa) turns out beautifully clear.
  2. Once the surface is clean, stir in a generous tablespoon of salt, reduce the heat to low, cover the pot with a lid, and let the lamb simmer gently for about two hours until the meat is meltingly tender and practically falling away from the bone.
  3. Peel the potatoes (cut in half if large) and drop them directly into the lamb broth 30 minutes before the meat finishes cooking. Once fork-tender, remove them with a slotted spoon and set aside in a covered bowl to keep warm.
  4. Remove the lamb from the pot, discard the bones, shred the meat into bite-sized pieces, and cover. Ladle the fatty top layer of the broth into a separate small saucepan, add the thinly sliced white onions and a generous amount of black pepper, and simmer for 7 minutes until softened.
  5. Bring the remaining lamb broth back to a rolling boil. Drop the zhaima sheets into the liquid in batches to prevent sticking, boil for 6-8 minutes (depends on the zhaima) until soft and slippery, then lift them out with a slotted spoon.
  6. Spread the hot zhaima sheets across a large platter, pile the shredded lamb in the center, and arrange the potatoes around the edges. Pour the hot, peppery onion gravy over the meat and noodles, and serve immediately with bowls of the remaining hot broth (sorpa) on the side.
